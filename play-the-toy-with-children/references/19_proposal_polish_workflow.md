# Proposal Polish Workflow

Use this reference for grant proposals, fellowship proposals, project proposals,
research statements, RFP responses, internal pitches, and proposal sections
inside a paper-to-funding workflow.

## Intake

Collect or infer:

- Proposal type, audience, funder/venue, page limit, deadline, and evaluation
  rubric.
- Current draft or source materials.
- Target ask: funding, approval, collaboration, compute time, data access, or
  research direction.
- Available evidence: data, code, preliminary results, figures, papers,
  user expertise, collaborators, and constraints.
- Desired stance: conservative, ambitious, exploratory, or reviewer-proof.

If the topic is broad or the field position is unclear, run the iterative
literature loop before polishing claims.

## Proposal Contract

A polished proposal should make these elements easy to find:

- One-sentence ask.
- Problem and why it matters.
- Gap in current work or practice.
- Central thesis, hypothesis, or design principle.
- Specific aims or work packages.
- Approach and methods for each aim.
- Preliminary evidence or feasibility argument.
- Expected outcomes and impact.
- Risks, alternatives, and decision gates.
- Timeline, milestones, deliverables, and success metrics.
- Team, resources, and fit when relevant.
- Reviewer objections and planned answers.

## Polish Passes

1. Compliance pass: align with the call, rubric, page limit, required sections,
   formatting, and eligibility constraints.
2. Argument pass: make the ask, gap, thesis, aims, and expected outcome form a
   single chain.
3. Specificity pass: replace vague ambition with measurable aims, datasets,
   model objects, experiments, milestones, and deliverables.
4. Evidence pass: attach local results, literature support, preliminary data,
   or explicit assumptions to each major claim.
5. Risk pass: state what could fail, how it will be detected, and what
   alternative path remains valuable.
6. Reviewer pass: simulate objections about novelty, feasibility, scope,
   evidence, budget, timeline, and team fit.
7. Style pass: make paragraphs direct, funder-facing, and contribution-forward
   without overstating unsupported results.

## Section Skeleton

Use or adapt this order unless the call imposes a template:

1. Title and one-sentence ask.
2. Problem, significance, and "why now."
3. Landscape/gap: what others have done and what remains open.
4. Central hypothesis or proposal thesis.
5. Aim 1: objective, method, evidence, milestone, risk.
6. Aim 2: objective, method, evidence, milestone, risk.
7. Aim 3 or integration plan when needed.
8. Expected outcomes, impact, and deliverables.
9. Timeline, resources, team fit, and broader use.

## Reviewer-Facing Ledger

Maintain a compact ledger:

- Claim
- Proposal section
- Evidence source
- Literature support
- Risk or objection
- Planned response
- Status: strong / adequate / weak / unsupported

Claims marked weak or unsupported should be softened, moved to future work, or
backed by more evidence before final polish.

## Output Contract

For proposal polish, return:

- Proposal diagnosis: strongest point, weakest risk, missing evidence.
- Revised spine: title, ask, thesis, aims, and expected outcome.
- Section-by-section edit plan or edited text, depending on the user's request.
- Reviewer objection matrix.
- Literature loop status: none / quick / full / monitor, with unresolved gaps.
- Next assets needed for slides or final submission.
